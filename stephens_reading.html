Contents

¤ Gladys M. Stephens to Mrs. A. T. Stephens, 06 July 1909

Writing from Seattle, Stephens describes the Alaska-Yukon-Pacific Exposition and the city of Seattle, then reviews her correspondence with friends and family and lists the contents of a package she plans to send to her mother.

¤ [Gladys M. Stephens to Mr. A. T. Stephens, 10 July 1909]

Stephens describes a multi-day visit to Yellowstone Park, where she drove a coach of four horses for 3 days. She characterizes her travel companions as "the Virginians" and "some Germans." She describes the Forestry Building and its exhibits at the 1909 Seattle World's Fair and notes that the Seattle World's Fair buildings will later be used by Seattle College. She also remarks on Seattle, which she describes as looking "new" and having a number of 12- and 14-story buildings.

¤ [Gladys M. Stephens to Mrs. A. T. Stephens, 14 July 1909]

Stephens writes that she will probably not travel to Alaska because staterooms for the voyage are in such high demand. She mentions telephoning the Goulds in Seattle, then describes her travel companion Mrs. Hughes and Mrs. Hughes' famous cousins, Mrs. Dana Gibson and Mrs. Waldorf Astor, as well as Mrs. Hughes' two sons and now-deceased husband.

¤ [Gladys M. Stephens to Mrs. A. T. Stephens, 08 August 1909]

Stephens tells her mother of the group's return to Seattle; anticipates a visit to a wealthy Texan's ranch; makes ambiguous references to what we would assume is news of or gossip about friends and acquaintances from home; mentions correspondence with a few other individuals; recounts being shown around by Uncle Alex; and mentions plans for a trip to Texas the following summer.

¤ [James J. McIntyre to Gladys M. Stevens, 13 August 1909]

Writing from Seattle, McIntyre pens an eight-page love letter to Stephens. The letter also describes McIntyre's life as a merchant marine, including his annual salary and his recent transfer from the Santa Clara to the North Western.

¤ [Cora L. Anderson to Mrs. A. T. Stephens, 19 August 1909]

Writing from Sherborn, Massachusetts, Cora Anderson, Gladys Stephens's cousin, tells her aunt about the birth of her new baby. She mentions having received a letter from Gladys while the latter was traveling in Colorado.

¤ [James J. McIntyre to Gladys M. Stephens, 21 August 1909]

McIntyre pens another long love letter to Stephens, discussing his life aboard ship, the difficulties of their long-distance relationship, and their attitudes toward courtship. He also mentions for the first time a two-year-old girl on the ship named Gladys, with whose family he strikes up an acquaintance.

¤ [James J. McIntyre to Gladys M. Stephens, 23 August 1909]

Writing during a late watch, McIntyre mentions the rough weather, mentions some pictures he plans to send Stephens, describes a visit with "little Gladys" and her mother, complains about the difficulty of carrying on a correspondence abord ship, and muses at length about love.

¤ [Gladys M. Stephens to Mrs. A. T. Stevens, 06 September 1909]

Awaiting a letter from her mother, Stephens asks whether something is wrong.

¤ [James J. McIntyre to Gladys M. Stephens, 13 September 1909]

In another long letter, McIntyre describe his ship's cargo and its race with another ship along Alaska's Inside Passage. As he often does in this correspondence, he laments the difficulties of a long-distance relationship conducted primarily through letters, and he muses about future trips that he and Stephens might take together.

¤ [Gladys M. Stephens to Mrs. A. T. Stephens, 22 September 1909]

Writing from El Paso, Stephens evaluates Adelaide's wedding purchases and fiance, and recounts her own experience with head lice, which she picked up in Mexico. Stephens also explains to her mother that she is staying in El Paso longer than she originally planned so she can witness the Taft-Diaz meeting on October 16th, 1909. She briefly explains the political tension in Mexico between President Diaz, Vice-President Reyes, and the general populace.

¤ [Gladys M. Stephens to Mrs. A. T. Stephens, 27 September, 1909]

Stephens inquires about various family members and friends and discusses Adelaide's wedding preparations.

¤ [James J. McIntyre to Gladys M. Stephens, 04 October 1909]

McIntyre discusses the current schedule and route of the S.S. Northwestern and details specifications of the Northwestern compared to the Santa Clara. He also recounts a visit with the Purser's sister in the Ketchikan area and recounts two fatal accidents in the Seattle area, which cause him to relect on his own past.

¤ [Gladys M. Stephens to Mrs. A. T. Stephens, 05 October 1909]

Writing from El Paso, Stephens mentions some social engagements, tells her mother about Adelaide's shopping for her wedding, and asks about pictures she sent previously, which were taken in San Diego.

¤ [James J. McIntyre to Gladys M. Stephens, 11 October 1909]

McIntyre tells Stephens about a visit with his mother and sister, mentioning that he told them how much he appreciates all of Stephens's help.

[Seattle,] 7/6 [190] 91
Dear Mama,

I have made my first trip to the Exposition Grounds. They are simply dreams of beauty. Such flowers—and such quantities of them! There is one place where there are thousands and thousands of white, lavender and pure yellow pansies. In one place there are veritable terraces of water with a falls between This expanse is surrounded by the most beautiful display of poppies I ever saw. Then there are flowers like those I brought home from Senior Prom.— gerhaniums [sic] —dwarf sweet peas—every kind of flowers imaginable. Talk about roses, there are so many here that I can't think what California will be like. I went today with the Virginians. The men were hunting up old friends here in the city. I have a lot of truck, which I am going to send by mail. There are four pictures of Yellowstone which are exquisite—they are as natural as pictures can be. My Princetonian got them for me, and paid a dollar for them. The packages of tea I got at the Japanese tea garden from the little Japanese girl. I ordered (through Hayden) a 5 cent can of condensed milk.

The buildings are [fine?] at the fair. One of the Virginian ladies said this fair is much superior to the St. Louis and others because of its compactness as well as its beauty. The buildings will be used later as college b'l'd'gs. Today we only visited those in the inner circle. The place is laid out in this way [See Figure 1.]


Figure 1. Layout of the fairgrounds.

The amusement street is called pay streak—a mining term.

The city has several sky scrapers.

There is a Hawaiian village with real bare natives. We are going to visit it later. There also [are] Esquimos and a village. I am sending you also the daily program. Have not found the Goulds yet.

This morning it was raining and I slept until 3 8.25. We went to the Expo. at 10.30—got back at 4.40. Some day we are going up Puget Sound. I can do quite a stunt at eating now— devoured a small steak for my breakfast—a dish of strawberries, one of oatmeal, two muffins and three pancakes—and a cup of coffee. Sunburn is wearing off a trifle since I have left the park. They have a line of cable cars here, and some odd big ones. The population has increased 150,000 in 2 years. I am getting to be quite a critic on buildings with two builders.

Havent [sic] done much on the writing stunt. I wrote Miss Childs a steamer letter, one to Gus, I think I wrote one to Roswel, and a dozen lines to Nat telling him that I was having a dandy time—never knew where I was going next and did not know when I'd be home; and hoped that he'd have as pleasant a summer as I was having. Found nothing at the P.O. Gee! but I'd like to hear. Will probably be here until the 20th perhaps the 24th. Am not sure as they don't say.

Hope you—all are well.


Love and kisses
Gladys

[Seattle,] 7/10/ [190] 9
Dear Pa,

I do not know what day we will leave here, but we'll leave soon. Will return to El Paso by way of California. I am decidedly tanned from riding up on the top seat with the driver in Yellowstone Park. I drove the coach 3 days. There were 4 horses and the coach weighs 2400 lbs. It was against the rules, but the driver let me.3 When we met another coach or any soldiers the driver took the reins. Talk about sore arms! And my nose, oh my nose! It was terribly burned, looked just like a beet. It has pealed [sic] now and begins to look more respectable.

With us are some charming Virginians and some Germans. We certainly have good times. They are all jolly.

The Exposition is fine In The Forestry b'l'd'g are some pretty big sticks. One is 74 ft. long and 4 1/2 ft. sq. There is a cross section of a tree which is 9 ft. in diam. There is a hollow tree trunk which will hold 50 peopl [sic] . The building is really mammouth [sic] . The columns are huge trees.4 I got what I hope will be a good picture of it. All the b'l'd'g's are fine and will be used by Seattle College later. Every one who has been to the other Expositions say that This is the best yet. The city is large and steadily increasing, but looks very new. There are lots of large bl'd'gs— plenty of 12 stories and several 14. There are some dandy hotels.

Will Ma be home soon?

You had best not ans. This as I will have left before an ans could come.


Love
Gladys

[Seattle] 8/8/'09
Mother dear,

We arrived in Seattle at 7.30 this morning. It was rather rough last night, but it was just from the wind. It was the only rough place we had coming back. We had a dandy time coming back.

As a souvenir I have the official list of the passangers [sic] who sailed from Seattle. It was posted by the Purser, and he gave it to me.9

I'd like to know what is the matter with auntie. I sent her cards from Yellowstone and some from here — among them a leather A.Y.P. card.10 I sent her some from Alaska. also. Almost every time I sent you one I sent her one.

2. I was not sick once while I was gone. I am enough fatter so that people here at the hotel spoke of it. I'm more tanned also.

A Texas judge who is worth 3 million 11 was on the ship coming back with his wife, two daughters and 2 grandsons. He owns a ranch in Texas which is about 12 miles square. They have invited me to visit them, and Uncle Alex wants me too [sic] . He may have a business motive. I will likely stop there for 2 or 3 days. He wants to take me to a real southern hunt. I can have a pony all for myself he said. It is an opportunity not to be lost, don't you agree?

3 We had a great time last night— our last on board. No one turned in before 12 some not until 2. I did at 12. It seems awfully funny to be on land again. They certainly were glad to see us here at the hotel.12

Well you never can tell what people will do. Walt certainly seems to be the limit. As for his mother she is the limit.

So you like the Thomases? What's that about Berenice? Do you believe it?

I fail to see the point of Mrs. Rich's joke. So the tree didn't bloom.

I did not send a card to Will Ward. I sent it to his mother!

4 He is a cheerful lier [sic] . I have not sent any more to the Dotens. Do not worry. I got a letter from Walt which I will enclose. In it he acknowledges one letter.

I did not have any hat on while I was gone. I'm sending a large envelope with one or too [sic] things I do not want lost.

I got your five letters this morning, and one from Walt, and one from Adelaide. I'm not sure whether Adelaide is going to Mexico or not.

The Mexican trip is all planned and I start home immediately on getting back. I'll be about one week late. U.A. has the day set for us to start to Mexico and all plans [O.K.?]

5 He wants me to see all the people whom he knows. He has told them about my coming for so long. I'm doing my best I assure you. I guess I'm succeeding fairly well. He is so tickled he does not know what to do. He is starting plans for next summer already.

We are "on" for the pictures.


Heaps of love
Neddy

The front side contains a 2.5-inch letterhead, a 6.75-inch body with blue lines spaced at .375-inch intervals, and a .25-inch bottom margin. The back side is unlined. An oval watermark, variably placed on each sheet and measuring approximately 2.375 x 2.625 inches when entirely visible, reads, in outline letters, "MERCHANTS | PRT'G CO | LURIE & LURIE." The letterhead contains, in the upper left-hand corner, a 1.375 x 2.4375-inch image of the Hotel Georgian facade. It should be noted that other letters in the collection, even as recently as the letters written by Gladys in July from the same hotel, are likewise composed on Hotel Georgian stationery, but some time in the interim of Gladys's excursion to Alaska, the Hotel Georgian modified its stationery slightly. The image is now an illustration rather than a photo, and depicts the hotel facade from a more diagonal angle (we see proportionally more of the side of the building. The font of the letterhead has also changed (tending toward more of a block style than the slanted script) and minor additions have been made. Near the top border of the paper, the hotel owner's names are printed ("D. Swinehart" and "Fred G. Caldwell"), and new qualifiers have been added under the "Hotel Georgian" moniker. The hotel stationery no longer includes a partially printed dateline ("Seattle, ________190____"), and so Gladys has handwritten the date ("8/8/.09") near the bottom, and slightly left of center, inside the bounded letterhead. On subsequent pages, she numbers the pages by hand, again within the boundaries of the letterhead, and in virtually the same place each time but not always in the same style (some page numbers are followed by periods, some by nothing, and one is underlined).

As I am now standing my last watch before arriving at Juneau and I am still a wondering and lonesome as usual I thought I would write a fiew [sic] which will probbely [sic] be the last chance to mail unless they is a boat farther north than Cordova which seldome [sic] happens It is now 1:30 a.m. as I am still on the same 12/4 as I was on Santa Clara and we should arrive in Juneau about noon and I do not know how long we will be there and of corse [sic] cannot say if I can get up to see about the pictures but will try!

As I said before Neddy dear I am writing on watch so please excuse pencil and a few finger marks you will I know tell Uncle A that we have 6 or 7 school teachers aboard this time and I think he could enjoy him self and Neddy I have found a sweet little girl aboard and her name is Gladys So you know she must be O.K. Explanatory Annotations

1. Because Gladys Stephens often uses letterhead with a form at the top for the dateline, we encode the printed text around her handwriting with a <supplied> element, but other printed parts of the letterhead are not encoded. They are described briefly in the manuscript descriptions following the letters and can be viewed in the facsimiles. [Back]

2. Similar numbers are penciled under the canceled postage stamp, or elsewhere, on several envelopes in the collection. The second number is circled. The letters from Gladys M. Stephens to Mr. and Mrs. A.T. Stephens dated between 07/06/1909 and 07/30/1909 appear to be sequentially numbered 94, 95, 96, 97, 98, and 99, suggesting that they are curatorial notations. [Back]

3. Gladys most likely drove a four-horse "Yellowstone Observation Wagon" used to take visitors on a "Grand Loop" of Yellowstone Park. Starting in 1884, the Yellowstone Park Transportation Company operated on a stage coach line that began at the Northern Pacific Railroad terminal in Cinnabar, Montana, connected to the hotel at Mammoth Hot Springs, and continued throughout the park. In his 1895 volume The Yellowstone Park, Hiram Martin Chittenden of the Army Corps of Engineers describes tourist transportation through the park as mostly by coach, ordinarily with four horses each (203). He comments that these coaches were a necessity because The points of interest are so scattered (Chittenden 274). [Back]

4. The Forestry Building served as a botanical and natural museum after the World's Fair concluded. It was torn down in 1930, at which point the log columns that Gladys describes had been damaged beyond repair by insects. The University of Washington Library's Special Collections Web site notes that the Forestry Building was sponsored by the State of Washington and was intended to showcase the state's forest resources. Architects Charles Saunders and George Lawton created a building that echoed the European style of Howard's buildings but which also incorporated the log-cabin idiom of early pioneer buildings. Featuring enormous unprocessed logs felled in Chehalis (now Gray's Harbor) County, the Forestry Building's grand colonnade and soaring interior spaces evoked the majesty of Washington's seemingly limitless forests and, not coincidentally, implied the great potential wealth they contained (University of Washington Library, Special Collections). [Back]

6. Mrs. Dana Gibson, born Irene Langhorne, was married to artist Charles Dana Gibson in 1895. Irene's sister Nancy Langhorne married British politician Waldorf Astor and later succeeded him as a Member of Parliament; she was the first woman to sit in the British House of Commons. Nancy and Irene are commonly credited as the source for the iconic Gibson Girl cartoons drawn by Charles Dana Gibson (Wolfe). [Back]

8. Similar numbers are penciled under the canceled postage stamp on several envelopes in the collection. The second number is circled. [Back]

9. The Purser on a cruise ship traditionally has several important responsibilities. They provide customer service to guests; they also handle money on board, for both the ship's crew and its guests—which may have involved buying luxury goods on credit and bringing them aboard. The Purser also helps guests with paperwork such as customs declarations. Pursers' duties are similar in definition to the ship's steward. So, given that a Purser would be in charge of all manners of record-keeping, both personally and financially, for all people on board, it makes sense that Gladys would have obtained this kind of souvenir from the Purser (Nautical Dictionary). [Back]

10. The "A.Y.P. cards" Gladys mentions here are probably postcards from the Alaska-Yukon-Pacific (A.Y.P.) exhibition. A.Y.P. postcards, a large collection of which are housed in Special Collections at the University of Washington Libraries, feature various images from the Alaska-Yukon-Pacific Exposition held in Seattle in 1909. Although the Exposition did employ an official photographer for the event, amateur photographers were permitted to take unlimited photos of the event (with certain restrictions) and so several different individuals and publishers made and sold postcards at the Exposition. Some of the postcards are in color and others are in black and white. It is interesting that Gladys says the card she sends to her aunt from Seattle is made of leather; leather postcards were a popular novelty item in the first decade of the twentieth century, but were actually banned by the United States Postal Service in the year 1909, so Gladys may have been one of the last citizens to mail one. [Back]

12. As is clear from previous letters, and from her comment here that "They certainly were glad to see us here at the hotel," Gladys—and apparently the other passengers on the excursion to Alaska—stayed at the Hotel Georgian before and after the voyage north, and it is a familiar place to the group. The Hotel Georgian was, in fact, a new hotel in Seattle when these visitors were staying there, built only one year before in 1908. It had seven floors and 108 rooms (Shannon). [Back]

18.  Since the French Revolution in the late 1700's, male theatre dancers were considered effeminate. Though McIntyre is referring to dance as a social practice with his friends, we can see how the dominant culture would have found dancing in general as a gender specific female acivity. When men participated, it was alongside women through ballroom dancing. We do not know if he attended these gatherings with only his male friends or if women would join their group, but in the following lines of McIntyre's letter, he mentions that his mother would have long talks with him about being a better boy. He seems to be referring to the idea that he would get into trouble after coming home from a party or dance ("Encouraging Male Participation in Dance"). [Back]

21. Though white wedding dresses were traditionally preferred in the United States and Great Britain since Queen Victoria's wedding in 1840, Emily Holt's 1901 Encyclopaedia of Etiquette allows "brides who are married before twelve o'clock, or who go directly from the altar to a train or boat" to "wear a becoming street dress of ladies' cloth, veiling, or silk, in a pale shade of blue" (Holt 238). Holt adds that if the bride wears this in place of the traditional white wedding gown, her hat is one of "the chief adjuncts of this toilette" (238). Based on Gladys's description of Adelaide's wedding attire in this letter and in her letter to her mother dated October 5, 1909, Adelaide seems to be preparing for a wedding that either takes place before noon or one from which she and her husband immediately depart after the ceremony (Holt 238). [Back]

Stephens's disapproving attitude of alcohol may reflect the growing temperance movement that was very strong in Boston, near where Stephens lived (Thomas 49-52). [Back]

34. Between 1883 and 1912, production of Ostrich feathers was signficantly ramped up in the United States, particularly the American South, West and Southwest, as a means of freeing American consumers from the import market dominated by South African production. Considered fashionable, the feathers combined with Agro-tourism to make California Ostrich Farms a popular destination during this period (Sarah Stein). [Back]

35.  A tramp engineer has little formal training, learns on the job, and frequently moves from job to job. In The Engineers' Review, I. C. Lloyd defends the tramp engineer as competent compared to the the traditional worker allied to one or two jobs, even though the tramp engineer also won't work in a place long enough to get married to the job. They like new scenes and experiences and won't take back talk from anybody ( Lloyd 18). The context of McIntyre's statement suggests that he is (and has been) the tramp engineer. In his letter to Gladys of 13 August 1909, he asks the company's superintendent engineer to transfer him from the Santa Clara to the Northwestern. Later in the current letter, he details the number of the Santa Clara's engineering crew, and he exhibits at least a neophyte familiarity with technical specifications and boiler operation. [Back]

36.  Tuxekan was renowned for its totem poles, and Klawock created a Totem Park to display authentic and replica totem poles, which McIntyre seems to anticipate here ("Visitor Information"). The preservation effort doesn't seem to be isolated, as a 1927 New York Times article laments the Alaskan Indians' scant regard for the ritual of the old tradition of totem-making and relates the need for funds for the preservation of these monuments ("Deserted Totems of Alaska"). [Totems at old Tuxekan]. [Back]

37.  [T]he first cannery in Alaska was built [in Klawock] by a San Francisco firm in 1878, and until 1920, it remained the only salmon cannery in the state ( "Visitor Information"). McIntyre may intend for his involvement with what at the time was leading industrialization to impress Gladys, as he later takes a boastful tone when explaining the specifications of the Northwestern in comparison to other ships. [The Cannery at Klawock]. [Back]

38.  A promotional travel guide published by the Alaska Steamship Company describes Ketchikan as a tourist destination, and the waterfalls represent not only the leisure of sightseeing but also the rapid growth and modernization of Ketchikan's infrastructure. The city has run pipes to the falls of the Ketchikan river which gives the citizens an opportunity to procure water direct from this cold and refreshing stream. It is said to be the best system in Alaska. Ketchikanites need have no fears of disease germs lurking in their drinking water. McIntyre and Palmer likely took what the guide calls a jaunt of two miles along the Ketchikan River to the city's intake, where [a] walk has been built near the bank of this winding stream and the tourist wends his way among the timber until the first falls are reached (A Trip to Wonderful Alaska). [Ketchikan in Wonderful Alaska]. [Back]

40.  The Locomotive reports that on 11 March 1909, [a] boiler exploded . . . on the steamer Weitchpec and Fireman Elmer Dennis was fatally scalded ("March, 1909" 171). The serious and fatal nature of boiler accidents is clearly conveyed in the volume's sizeable monthly catalogues. Additionally, a 1916 issue of The Popular Science Monthly laments that [a]t present the life of coal-passers and engineers is one of extreme hazard. To maintain pressure in the various compartments in the engine room, it is necessary for the men to enter or leave by means of air locks . . . This method of entrance and exit is, therefore, very slow, and should the compartment become suddenly filled with steam or noxious gases, all the men at work could not gain the outer air in time to save their lives ("Saving Men from Scalding Steam in Steamship Engine Rooms" 254). [Back]

41.  The September 23 issue of The Seattle Star reports that a big Pope-Toledo automobile crashed through the bridge railing at Fourth av. S. and Lane st. shortly before 6 o'clock this morning. It crashed to the tideflats below and turned turtle, pinning its occupants beneath. The auxiliary gas tank caught fire upon the vehicle's crash. The driver, Henry Hiser, managed to extricate himself and another passenger, but he scurried from the scene and was later placed under arrest ( "Two Girls Burned and Crushed Beyond Human Resemblance"). The October 1 issue of The Seattle Republican further explains that Hiser hauled a seventeen-year-old girl from saloon to saloon all night long and drank until he was almost beastly drunk and then started over a dangerous road to the city at a speed of a mile a minute ("Henry Hiser"). McIntyre seems to admit here that he used to practice a similar lifestyle. In his letter to Gladys of 13 August 1909, he expresses his committment to abstaining from drinking and the repugnance of the idea of Gladys spending time with the drinking Class. [Back]
