¤ [Gladys M. Stephens to Mrs. A. T. Stephens, 06 July 1909]

Writing from Seattle, Stephens describes the Alaska-Yukon-Pacific Exposition and the city of Seattle, then reviews her correspondence with friends and family and lists the contents of a package she plans to send to her mother.

¤ [Gladys M. Stephens to Mr. A. T. Stephens, 10 July 1909]

Gladys Stephens describes a multi-day visit to Yellowstone Park where she drove a coach of four horses for 3 days. She mentions her travel companions "the Virginians" and "some Germans." She describes the exhibits in the Forestry Building at the 1909 Seattle World's Fair. She describes the Forestry Building and comments that the Seattle World's Fair buildings will later be used by Seattle College. She also remarks on Seattle, which she describes as looking "new" but having a number of 12- and 14-story buildings.

¤ [Gladys M. Stephens to Mrs. A. T. Stephens, 14 July 1909]

Gladys says that she is not going to Alaska because staterooms for the voyage are in such high demand. She mentions telephoning the Goulds in Seattle, then talks about her travel companion Mrs. Hughes. She particularly describes Mrs. Hughes' famous cousins Mrs. Dana Gibson and Mrs. Waldorf Astor as well as Mrs. Hughes' two sons and now-deceased husband.

¤ [Gladys M. Stephens to Mrs. A.T. Stephens, 08 August 1909]

Gladys tells her mother of the group's return to Seattle; anticipates a visit to a wealthy Texan's ranch; makes ambiguous references to what we would assume is news or gossip of friends and acquaintances from home; mentions correspondence with a few other individuals; and brags about being shown around by Uncle Alex and the "plans for next summer" already in the works.

¤ [James J. McIntyre to Gladys M. Stevens, 13 August 1909]

Writing from Seattle, J. J. McIntyre crafted an 8 page love letter to Gladys M. Stevens. The letter describes certain details about McIntyre's life as a merchant marine, including his annual salary and his transfer from the Santa Clara to the North Western.

¤ [Cora L. Anderson to Mrs. A. T. Stephens, 19 August 1909]

Writing from Sherborn, Massachusetts, Cora Anderson, Gladys's cousin, tells her aunt about the birth of her new baby. She mentions having received a letter from Gladys while the latter was traveling in Colorado.

Guard watch; weather; pictures; Little Gladys

¤ [Gladys M. Stephens to Mrs. A.T. Stevens, 06 September 1909]

Gladys is waiting for mother's (A.T. Stevens) letter, is something wrong? Alaska ring arrived. Pillow.

¤ [James J. McIntyre to Gladys M. Stephens, 13 September 1909]

Black Ball line; Alaska; passengers; freight.

¤ [Gladys M. Stephens to Mrs. A. T. Stephens, 22 September 1909]

Writing from El Paso, Gladys evaluates Adelaide's wedding purchases and fiance, as well as Gladys's experience with head lice, which she picked up in Mexico. Gladys also explains to her mother that she is staying in El Paso longer than she originally planned so she can witness the Taft-Diaz meeting on October 16th, 1909. She briefly explains the political tension in Mexico between President Diaz, Vice-President Reyes, and the general populace. Though Gladys is definitely writing from El Paso, her exact location within the city is unknown. Her letter is on stationary from the Office of the Treasurer in Santa Cruz, California, but the postmark indicates she mailed the letter from El Paso. In her letter, she mentions how her Uncle wants her to extend her stay in El Paso, which suggests she might be staying with him, but then she discusses her extensive time spent with Adelaide, which suggests she is staying with her.

¤ [Gladys M. Stephens to Mrs. A.T. Stephens, 27 September, 1909]

A letter from Gladys M. Stephens to Mrs. A.T. Stephens on the 27th of September, 1909 in which Gladys discusses her various relationships and correspondences, including her plans with Adelaide and a number of family associations back home.

¤ [James J. McIntyre to Gladys M. Stephens, 04 October 1909]

McIntyre relates the ship schedule and route for the S.S. Northwestern and spending time with the Purser's sister in the Ketchikan area. He discusses pictures received from Gladys, and details specifications of the Northwestern compared to the Santa Clara. McIntyre recounts two auto accidents in the Seattle area and reflects on their meaning for him.

¤ [Gladys M. Stephens to Mrs. A. T. Stephens, 05 October 1909]

Writing from El Paso, Stephens mentions some social engagements, tells her mother about Adelaide's shopping for her wedding, and asks about pictures she sent previously, which were taken in San Diego.

¤ [James J. McIntyre to Gladys M. Stephens, 11 October 1909]

Letter from James J. McIntyre to Gladys telling how he missed a ship and how he has recently been telling his mother how much he appreciates all of Gladys's help.

7/6 9
Dear Mama,

I have made my first trip to the Exposition Grounds. They are simply dreams of beauty. Such flowers—and such quantities of them! There is one place where there are thousands and thousands of white, lavender and pure yellow pansies. In one place there are veritable terraces of water with a falls between This expanse is surrounded by the most beautiful display of poppies I ever saw. Then there are flowers like those I brought home from Senior Prom.— gerhaniums—dwarf sweet peas—every kind of flowers imaginable. Talk about roses, there are so many here that I can't think what California will be like. I went today with the Virginians. The men were hunting up old friends here in the city. I have a lot of truck, which I am going to send by mail. There are four pictures of Yellowstone which are exquisite—they are as natural as pictures can be. My Princetonian got them for me, and paid a dollar for them. The packages of tea I got at the Japanese tea garden from the little Japanese girl. I ordered (through Hayden) a 5 cent can of condense milk.

The buildings are [fine?] at the fair. One of the Virginian ladies said this fair is much superior to the St. Louis and others because of its compactness as well as its beauty. The buildings will be used later as college b'l'd'gs. Today we only visited those in the inner circle. The place is laid out in this way


Layout of the fairgrounds.
The amusement street is called pay streak—a mining term.

The city has several skyscrapers.

There is a Hawaiian village with real bare natives. We are going to visit it later. There also Esquimos and a village. I am sending you also the daily program. Have not found the Goulds yet.

This morning it was raining and I slept until 3 8.25. We went to the Expo. at 10.30—got back at 4.40. Some day we are going up Puget Sound. I can do quite a stunt at eating now— devoured a small steak for my breakfast—a dish of strawberries, one of oatmeal, two muffins and three pancakes—and a cup of coffee. Sunburn is wearing off a trifle since I have left the park. They have a line of cable cars here, and some odd big ones. The population has increased 150,000 in 2 years. I am getting to be quite a critic on buildings with two builders.

Havent done much on the writing stunt. I wrote Miss Childs a steamer letter, one to Gus, I think I wrote one to Roswel, and a dozen lines to Nat telling him that I was having a dandy time—never knew where I was going next and did not know when I'd be home; and hoped that he'd have as pleasant a summer as I was having. Found nothing at the P.O. Gee! but I'd like to hear. Will probably be here until the 20th perhaps the 24th. Am not sure as they don't say.

Hope you—all are well.


Love and kisses
Gladys

7/10/ 9
Dear Pa,

I do not know what day we will leave here, but we'll leave soon. Will return to El Paso by way of California. I am decidedly [tanned?] from riding up on the top seat with the driver in Yellowstone Park. I drove the coach 3 days. There were 4 horses and the coach weighs 2400 lbs. It was against the rules, but the driver let me.2 When we met another coach or any soldiers the driver took the reins. Talk about sore arms! And my nose, oh my nose! It was terribly burned, looked just like a beet. It has pealed [sic] now and begins to look more respectable.

With us are some charming Virginians and some Germans. We certainly have good times. They are all jolly.

The Exposition is fine In The Forestry b'l'd'g are some pretty big "sticks". One is 74 ft long and 4 1/2 ft sq. There is a cross section of a tree which is 9 ft. in diam. There is a hollow tree trunk which will hold 50 peopl [sic] . The building is really mammouth [sic] . The columns are huge trees.3 I got what I hope will be a good picture of it. All the b'l'd'g's are fine. and will be used by Seattle College later. Every one who has been to the other Expositions say that This is the best yet. The city is large and steadily increasing, but looks very new. There are lots of large bl'd'gs— plenty of 12 stories and several 14. There are some dandy hotels.

Will Ma be home soon?

You had best not ans. This as I will have left before an ans could come.


Love
Gladys

Mother Dear,

We arrived in Seattle at 7:20 this morning. It was rather rough last night, but it was just from the wind. It was the only rough place we had coming back. We had a dandy time coming back.

As a souvenir I have the official list of the passangers who sailed from Seattle. It was posted by the Purser, and he gave it to me.8

I'd like to know what is the matter with auntie. I sent her cards from Yellowstone and some from here — among them a leather A.Y.P. card.9 I sent her some from Alaska. also. Almost every time I sent you one I sent her one.

I was not sick once while I was gone. I am enough fatter so that the people here at the hotel spoof of it. I'm more tanned also.

A Texas judge who is worth 3 million 10 was on the ship coming back with his wife, two daughters and 2 grandsons. He owns a ranch in Texas which is about 12 miles square. They have invited me to visit them, and Uncle Alex wants me [too?] . He may have a business motive. I will likely stop there for 2 or 3 days. He wants to take me to a real southern hunt. I can have a pony all for myself he said. It is an opportunity not to be lost, don't you agree?

We had a great time last night- our last on board. No one turned in before 12 some not until 2. I did at 12. It seems awfully funny to be on land again. They certainly were glad to see us here at the hotel.11

Well you never can tell what people will do. Walt certainly seems to be the limit. As for his mother she is the limit.

So you like the Thomases? What's that about Berenice? Do you believe it?

I fail to see the point of Mrs. Rich's joke. So the tree didn't bloom.

I did not send a card to Will Ward. I sent it to his mother !

He is a cheerful lier. I have not sent any more to the Dotens. Do not worry. I got a letter from Walt which I will enclose. In it he acknowledges one letter.

I did not have any hat on while I was gone. I'm sending a large envelope with one or too things I do not want lost.

I got your five letters this morning, and one from Walt, and one from Adelaide. I'm not sure whether Adelaide is going to Mexico or not.

The Mexican trip is all planned and I start home immediately on getting back. I'll be about one week late. U.A. has the day set for us to start to Mexico and all plans [O.K.?]

He wants me to see all the people whom he knows. He has told them about my coming for so long. I'm doing my best I assure you. I guess I'm succeeding fairly well. He is so tickled he does not know what to do. He is starting plans for next summer already. We are "on" for the pictures. Heaps of love Neddy

This original letter contains five loose sheets of cream-colored Hotel Georgian letterhead stationery measuring 6 x 9.5 inches. They have been folded in half and folded together. The front side contains a 2.5-inch letterhead, a 6.75-inch body with blue lines spaced at .375-inch intervals, and a .25-inch bottom margin. The back side is unlined. An oval watermark, variably placed on each sheet and measuring approximately 2.375 x 2.625 inches when entirely visible, reads, in outline letters, "MERCHANTS | PRT'G CO | LURIE & LURIE." The letterhead contains, in the upper left-hand corner, a 1.375 x 2.4375-inch image of the Hotel Georgian facade. It should be noted that other letters in the collection, even as recently as the letters written by Gladys in July from the same hotel, are likewise composed on Hotel Georgian stationery, but some time in the interim of Gladys's excursion to Alaska, the Hotel Georgian modified its stationery slightly. The image is now an illustration rather than a photo, and depicts the hotel facade from a more diagonal angle (we see proportionally more of the side of the building. The font of the letterhead has also changed (tending toward more of a block style than the slanted script) and minor additions have been made. Near the top border of the paper, the hotel owner's names are printed ("D. Swinehart" and "Fred G. Caldwell"), and new qualifiers have been added under the "Hotel Georgian" moniker. The hotel stationery no longer includes a partially printed dateline ("Seattle, ________190____"), and so Gladys has handwritten the date ("8/8/.09") near the bottom, and slightly left of center, inside the bounded letterhead. On subsequent pages, she numbers the pages by hand, again within the boundaries of the letterhead, and in virtually the same place each time but not always in the same style (some page numbers are followed by periods, some by nothing, and one is underlined).

2. Gladys most likely drove a 4-horse "Yellowstone Observation Wagon," which was a type of stagecoach used to take visitors on a "Grand Loop" of Yellowstone Park. Starting in 1884, the Yellowstone Park Transportation Company operated on a stage coach line that began at the Northern Pacific Railroad terminal in Cinnabar, Montana, connected to the hotel at Mammoth Hot Springs, and continued throughout the park. In his 1895 volume The Yellowstone Park, Hiram Martin Chittenden of the Army Corps of Engineers describes "tourist transportation" through the park as "mostly by coach, ordinarily with four horses each" (203). He comments that these coaches were a "necessity" because "The points of interest are so scattered" (274). ( Department of the Interior, U.S. National Park Service. Chittenden, Hiram Martin. ) [Back]

3. The Forestry Building served as a botanical and natural museum after the World's Fair concluded. It was torn down in 1930, at which point the log columns that Gladys describes had been damaged beyond repair by insects. The Forestry Building is described in detail on the University of Washington Library's Special Collections web site as being "sponsored by the State of Washington and was intended to showcase the state's forest resources. Architects Charles Saunders and George Lawton created a building that echoed the European style of Howard's buildings but which also incorporated the log-cabin idiom of early pioneer buildings. Featuring enormous unprocessed logs felled in Chehalis (now Gray's Harbor) County, the Forestry Building's grand colonnade and soaring interior spaces evoked the majesty of Washington's seemingly limitless forests and, not coincidentally, implied the great potential wealth they contained." (University of Washington Library, Special Collections) [Back]

8. The Purser on a cruise ship traditionally has several important responsibilities. 9. The "A.Y.P. cards" Gladys mentions here are probably postcards from the Alaska-Yukon-Pacific (A.Y.P.) exhibition. A.Y.P. postcards, a large collection of which are housed in Special Collections at the University of Washington Libraries, feature various images from the Alaska-Yukon-Pacific Exposition in Seattle in 1909. Although the Exposition did employ an official photographer for the event, amateur photographers were permitted to take unlimited photos of the event (with certain restrictions) and so several different individuals and publishers made and sold postcards at the Exposition. Some of the postcards are in color (and so may have been manipulated) and others are in black and white. It is interesting that Gladys says the card she sends to her aunt from Seattle is made of leather; leather postcards were a popular novelty item in the first decade of the twentieth century, but were actually banned by the United States Postal Service in the year 1909- so Gladys may have been one of the last citizens to mail one.
