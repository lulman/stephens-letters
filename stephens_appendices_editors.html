About the Editors

Robert (Trey) Eugene Conatser is a first-year doctoral student specializing in British Romantic literature and digital humanities in the Department of English at The Ohio State University. Trey's scholarly writing has been published in New Media & Society and The CEA Critic, and his poetry and other writing has appeared in journals such as Seneca Review, New York Quarterly, Copper Nickel, Mid-American Review, and Prairie Schooner. Between his MA and PhD programs, he taught composition, research and argumentative writing, creative writing, and technical writing at Ivy Tech Community College of Indiana in Indianapolis.

Jonathan H. Holmes is a PhD student whose research interests include Renaissance drama and poetry. His recent work has involved character theory and early modern dramatic representations of patience, historiographic research on The Taming of the Shrew, and an analysis of Paradise Lost through the lens of indigenous decolonization theory. He is also interested in web design, composition praxis, and the intersections of early modern rhetoric and poetics.

William C. Kurlinkus is a second-year doctoral student and Susan L. Huntington Fellow specializing in Rhetoric, Composition, and Literacy at The Ohio State University. His scholarly interests lie at the intersection of technological, rhetorical, and stylistic theory. His current research focuses on discourse communities with alternative (non-logos based) technological consubstantialities (technophobes, retro-fetishists, etc.)—how they form, develop aesthetics, compose, select histories, gatekeep, and communicate/clash with communities with opposing techno-logics. His work on the cross-over between classical rhetoric, style, and technology can be found in the forthcoming collection Style=Composition.

Jonathan Paul Leidheiser-Stoddard is currently a PhD student at the Ohio State University in the Near Eastern Languages and Cultures department, specializing in the Hebrew Bible. Jonathan has also served as an instructor, teaching the Mythologies of Ancient Egypt and Mesopotamia.

C. Marie Meza is currently a MA/Ph.D student studying English Literature at The Ohio State University. Carmen's research interests include Feminist Critical Theory, Renaissance Drama, and Gender Studies. Prior to attending OSU, she graduated with highest Honors from The University of Texas at El Paso with a degree in English Literature and Women's Studies. Carmen has taught writing courses at OSU since 2010 and aspires to teach at the college level after graduation..

Jennifer L. Michaels is a doctoral student in Rhetoric, Composition, and Literacy and Digital Media Studies. Between her MA and MFA programs, she taught composition for three years as a lecturer at the Sweetland Writing Center at The University of Michigan.

Victoria M. Muñoz is currently a graduate student at The Ohio State University. Her interests lie in Renaissance literature with a concentration on early modern women writers.

Ashley Cole Owens is a second-year Ph.D. student specializing in 19th Century American Literature at The Ohio State University. Ashley's research focuses on American literary history rather broadly, and she is interested primarily in the 19th century American literary marketplace and emerging distinctions of "highbrow" and "lowbrow" literary culture which gave shape to the American canon. Ashley recently won the graduate student award for a paper given at the New York College English Association's conference on "Literature and Feeling." She received her Masters degree from Ohio State in 2010 and a Bachelor of Arts in English with honors from Vanderbilt University in 2007. For the 2011-2012 year, Ashley is serving as the Graduate Administrative Associate for the Undergraduate Research Office at Ohio State. During her first three years of graduate school, she was a Graduate Teaching Associate in the English department. In addition to her teaching experience at Ohio State, Ashley taught a humanities course for Duke University's T.I.P. Program, a summer academic camp for gifted middle school students, in the summers of 2009 and 2011.

H. Lewis Ulman, Principal Editor, is Associate Professor of English at The Ohio State University and Director of Digital Media Studies in the Department of English. He has edited The Minutes of the Aberdeen Philosophical Society, 1758–1773 (Aberdeen University Press, 1990) and, with Dennis Quon, “Semiotics in Eighteenth-century Aberdeen: Thomas Gordon’s Contributions to the Aberdeen Philosophical Society” (Studies on Voltaire and the Eighteenth-Century 317 (1994): 57–115). He is also the author of Things, Thoughts, Words and Actions: The Problem of Language in Late Eighteenth-Century British Rhetorical Theory (Southern Illinois University Press, 1994). Since 2005, he has worked with undergraduate and graduate students in textual editing courses to create a series of electronic textual editions of nineteenth- and early twentieth-century American manuscripts in the Rare Books and Manuscripts Library at Ohio State. Beginning with an NEH Digital Humanities Start-Up Grant awarded in 2008, he has collaborated with Melanie Schlosser, metadata librarian at Ohio State University Libraries, to build a model of preservation for distributed, multimodal digital humanities projects. He is delighted to be working at a time when manuscript, print, and digital media can be studied in relationship to one another, allowing him and his students to learn more about all three media than they could by studying any one in isolation.

Rachel M. Waymel Rachel Waymel is a first-year PhD student at the Ohio State University specializing in medieval English literature. Her research interests include how cultural elements such as myth and ritual practices are used to shape regional and national identity in medieval England, particularly in the West Midlands and in response to waves of invasion. Her recent presentations include "From Innuendo to Insult: How Cultural Landscapes Affect the Presentation and Prevalence of Humor in the Norse-Icelandic Sagas" at the 45th International Congress on Medieval Studies at Kalamazoo in 2010, "Argante of Areley Kings: Multicultural Layering and Regional Nationalism in Layamon's Brut" at the Ohio Medieval Colloquium in 2010, and "Haleweiye vs. Herbed Wine: Healing Traditions and Regional Nationalism in in Layamon's Brut" at the Medieval Association of the Midwest Conference in 2011.

Marion Aikens Wolfe is a PhD student at The Ohio State University, focusing on Rhetoric and Composition. She has taught first- and second-year writing courses and currently works as a Writing Program Administrator for the First-year Writing Program.
